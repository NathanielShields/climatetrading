Datasets & data contracts

Every adapter conforms to the same two-timestamp contract so the backtest cannot accidentally peek at data it could not have seen at decision time.

The data contract

observation_ts

The wall-clock time the world was in this state. For a USDA Crop Progress row this is the Sunday week-end. For NDVI it's the composite mid-date. For futures it's the trade date. For an ERA5 reanalysis it's the day described, even if the file was published months later.

available_ts

The earliest wall-clock time a trader could actually have seen this row. USDA Crop Progress = Mon 4 PM ET. NDVI 16-day composite = mid-date + 7 days. ERA5 reanalysis used as a stand-in for forecast = same day. The engine filters every row to available_ts ≤ today.

Why it matters. Many "drought premium" backtests in the literature use ERA5 as if it were a real-time observation, which silently encodes a 1–6 day look-ahead. This system makes the lag explicit per source, so you can swap a real-time NRT product in later (and tighten the lag column) without rewriting the engine.

Geographic scope

Iowa as the corn-belt proxy

All point-based sources (Open-Meteo, ERA5) are evaluated at the Iowa centroid 42.03°N, 93.58°W. State-level sources (USDA NASS) use the Iowa state aggregate. Tile-based sources (MODIS) average tiles h10v04 and h11v04, which together cover essentially all of Iowa.

Iowa was chosen because it's the single largest U.S. corn-producing state and historically the most directly tied to CBOT corn vol regimes during summer.

Easy to extend

Adding Illinois, Indiana, or a multi-state composite is mechanical: pass a different region key into real_data.build_inputs(). The adapters parameterise on region; the signal weights are global. A serious production version would weight states by the USDA Iowa/Illinois/Indiana production share for the harvest year being modelled.
